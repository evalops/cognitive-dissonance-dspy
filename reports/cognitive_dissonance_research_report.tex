\begin{abstract}
Cognitive Dissonance DSPy is a research prototype that attempts to resolve
formalizable disagreements between language-model agents through proof rather
than conversational arbitration. The system combines DSPy-based claim handling,
natural-language-to-formal-spec translation, necessity-driven proof discovery,
hybrid SMT and Coq proving, and a trust-aware proof-status model that
distinguishes machine-checked results from weaker forms of evidence.

This report evaluates the repository as a technical research artifact rather
than as a demo. Its central finding is that the project's most important
contribution is epistemic discipline: the codebase does not treat generated
formal text, compiled proof scripts, solver outputs, and independently checked
proofs as equivalent. The repository now presents a credible architecture, real
CI coverage for \texttt{coqc}/\texttt{coqchk}, explicit persistence semantics,
and a growing reproducibility story, while still showing major open problems in
translation breadth, benchmark maturity, and semantic calibration.
\end{abstract}

\section*{Executive Summary}

\begin{summarybox}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Core idea.} Route formalizable agent disagreements into a proof
  pipeline and use proof outcomes, not debate, as the resolution mechanism.
  \item \textbf{Strongest contribution.} A normalized trust model that
  distinguishes machine-checked, SMT-derived, assumption-based, and
  inconclusive outcomes.
  \item \textbf{Distinctive technique.} Necessity-based proof discovery that
  resolves some claims through mathematical structure before broader search.
  \item \textbf{Current status.} The repository has green local tests, a green
  multi-version CI matrix, a Coq smoke job, cache-schema versioning, and a much
  cleaner engineering baseline than a typical proof-integrated demo.
  \item \textbf{Main gaps.} Pattern-driven translation, heuristic semantic
  bridging, and the lack of a rigorous benchmark protocol remain the dominant
  research limitations.
\end{itemize}
\end{summarybox}

\section{Introduction}

Multi-agent LLM systems often disagree. In most deployed or experimental
systems, those disagreements are handled through debate, ranking, confidence
aggregation, or arbitration prompts. Those methods are acceptable when the
underlying issue is genuinely subjective or underspecified. They are much less
appropriate when the disputed claim is formal and decidable.

If two agents disagree about whether \texttt{2 + 2 = 4}, whether a factorial
claim is correct, or whether a simple software property holds, a confidence-
weighted debate is the wrong instrument. The correct instrument is some form of
formal verification. Cognitive Dissonance DSPy is built around that thesis:
when a dispute is formalizable, stop arguing and attempt to prove it
\cite{repo_readme,fv_readme}.

\section{Research Questions}

This report evaluates the repository against four practical research questions.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{RQ1:} Does the architecture provide a coherent route from agent
  disagreement to proof-backed resolution?
  \item \textbf{RQ2:} Does the implementation distinguish clearly between
  strong and weak proof evidence?
  \item \textbf{RQ3:} Does the repository support a credible validation and
  reproducibility story?
  \item \textbf{RQ4:} What research gaps remain before the framework can
  support stronger empirical claims?
\end{enumerate}

\section{System Overview}

\subsection{Conceptual Pipeline}

At a high level, the repository implements the following workflow:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item DSPy agents extract beliefs or claims from source texts.
  \item The system detects cognitive dissonance or contradiction between those
  claims.
  \item Claims are classified into mathematical, algorithmic, software,
  physical, subjective, or unverifiable categories.
  \item Formalizable claims are translated or routed into proof targets.
  \item The proof stack attempts resolution through necessity analysis, hybrid
  SMT reasoning, or Coq-based proving and checking.
  \item Results are normalized into a stable proof-status vocabulary and then
  used to decide whether the framework should resolve deterministically,
  probabilistically, or diagnostically.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Core Technical Contributions}

\subsection{Trust-Aware Proof Semantics}

The strongest design decision in the repository is the refusal to flatten all
proof-looking outcomes into a single notion of success. The normalized
\texttt{ProofStatus} model in \nolinkurl{formal_verification/types.py} includes
machine-checked, SMT-proved, derived, refuted, formalized-but-unproved,
compiled-but-unchecked, checker-failed, timeout, and unavailable outcomes
\cite{types}.

This matters because it prevents the system from overstating certainty. In
particular:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Assumption-bearing Coq specifications are rejected before execution and
  treated as formalized but unproved.
  \item A compiled proof is not treated as ground truth until the independent
  proof checker succeeds.
  \item Solver-derived successes can inform resolution without being conflated
  with independently checked proof objects.
\end{itemize}

As a research contribution, this is more important than any single theorem the
system proves. It establishes an explicit evidence hierarchy for
proof-integrated agent systems.

\subsection{Necessity-Based Proof Discovery}

The necessity prover is the most distinctive algorithmic idea in the codebase.
Rather than launching generic search immediately, it tries to determine what
\emph{must} follow from mathematical structure. The implementation currently
handles:

\begin{itemize}
  \item arithmetic equalities and inequalities
  \item factorial claims
  \item Fibonacci claims
  \item GCD claims
  \item closed-form summation claims
  \item identity-style statements such as \texttt{n + 0 = n}
\end{itemize}

This is not a universal theorem prover. It is a routing and proof-construction
layer for domains where mathematical necessity can be recognized cheaply and
interpreted clearly. Its research value lies in the claim that some disputes
should be resolved by structure-aware proving before broader search begins.

\subsection{Hybrid Verification Stack}

The detector supports several verification modes:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Coq-only proving
  \item Z3 plus Coq hybrid solving
  \item necessity-enhanced proving on top of either base prover
  \item automated repair via lemma discovery
\end{itemize}

This arrangement matters because different claims are amenable to different
backends. Arithmetic or constraint-heavy claims often fit SMT reasoning.
Others benefit from theorem-oriented proof scripts or proof-structure
synthesis. The normalized status model lets these backends coexist without
pretending they all produce the same kind of evidence.

\subsection{Semantic Bridging of Subjective Language}

The semantic bridge attempts to extract objective targets from language that
would normally be dismissed as purely subjective. Terms such as
\texttt{efficient}, \texttt{secure}, \texttt{reliable}, \texttt{readable}, and
\texttt{scalable} are mapped to candidate objective components such as
execution time, complexity, error rate, input validation, memory safety, or
code complexity \cite{semantic_bridge}.

This is a useful research direction because many practical disagreements contain
both subjective framing and objective substructure. The bridge does not solve
the semantics problem completely, but it gives the framework a path for
converting some qualitative disputes into partially measurable ones.

\subsection{Repair, Caching, and Strategy Learning}

The repository also contains a second layer of research ambition:

\begin{itemize}
  \item proof-failure analysis and lemma suggestion
  \item on-disk proof caching with schema versioning
  \item proof-learning persistence for historical attempts and strategy
  prediction
\end{itemize}

Taken together, these pieces point toward an adaptive verification environment
that not only proves claims but also learns from previous proving behavior and
avoids repeating stale or redundant work.

\subsection{Repository-Reported Metrics}

The repository documentation reports several performance and success-rate
figures, including:

\begin{itemize}
  \item 15 claims with an 80\% success rate
  \item average proof time of roughly 179.7ms
  \item proof-caching speedups on repeated proofs
  \item strong arithmetic and mathematical-function success rates
\end{itemize}

These values are useful as research signals, but they should be treated as
repository-reported benchmarks rather than independently audited experimental
results. The engineering foundation is improving, but a mature empirical story
still needs fixed datasets, archived benchmark commands, seed control, and
clear reporting of machine-checked versus weaker proof outcomes.

\section{Findings}

\subsection{Finding 1: The Framework Raises the Standard of Evidence}

The repository's clearest success is that it upgrades the standard of evidence
for a subset of agent disagreements. The system no longer has to choose between
``pure prompt debate'' and ``pretending every formal-looking output is a
proof.'' It can represent multiple levels of proof confidence explicitly.

\subsection{Finding 2: The Architecture Is Research-Meaningful}

The project sits at a useful intersection of:

\begin{itemize}
  \item multi-agent reasoning
  \item formal verification
  \item theorem proving
  \item hybrid symbolic-probabilistic systems
\end{itemize}

The repository is strongest where these threads are integrated into a coherent
resolution policy rather than demonstrated independently.

\subsection{Finding 3: The Reproducibility Story Is Becoming Credible}

Schema-version handling, Coq smoke tests in CI, explicit proof-status
semantics, and a steadily improving code-quality baseline materially strengthen
the repository as a research artifact. These are the kinds of features that
allow the system to be trusted across revisions, not just during a one-time
demo.

\subsection{Finding 4: Translation and Benchmarking Remain the Main Gaps}

The project still relies heavily on pattern-driven translation and repo-level
benchmark claims. This is the main gap between a strong prototype and a
research result that can support stronger comparative or scientific claims.

\section{Limitations}

\subsection{Pattern-Driven Translation}

The natural-language-to-formal-spec translation layer remains template-heavy.
This works for arithmetic and a constrained set of algorithmic or
software-property claims, but it limits generality and makes coverage sensitive
to surface phrasing.

\subsection{Heuristic Semantic Bridging}

The semantic bridge is useful, but it is still heuristic. Its objectivity
levels, confidence scores, and generated verification targets are hand-crafted.
They are not yet backed by a formal semantics or a calibration study.

\subsection{Incomplete Benchmark Story}

The repository includes examples, tests, and reported metrics, but it does not
yet present a benchmark protocol with the level of rigor expected for a mature
research evaluation. In particular, the project still needs:

\begin{itemize}
  \item fixed benchmark inputs
  \item archived benchmark outputs
  \item ablation studies
  \item separate reporting for machine-checked versus weaker proof outcomes
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Remaining Repository Normalization Work}

The codebase is much cleaner than before, but line-length lint debt remains.
This is not the central scientific issue, but it still affects maintainability
and outside reproducibility.

\section{Threats to Validity}

\subsection{Construct Validity}

The system's strongest results apply to claims that are already formalizable or
close to formalizable. Measured success may therefore say as much about routing
and pattern coverage as it does about general open-domain reasoning.

\subsection{Internal Validity}

Some reported performance metrics come from repository documentation rather than
from a preserved benchmark harness. Without frozen inputs and archived outputs,
their stability across revisions is hard to assess.

\subsection{External Validity}

The framework is currently strongest on arithmetic, first-order fragments,
structured software properties, and heuristic decompositions of subjective
claims. It should not yet be generalized to the full space of agent
disagreements.

\section{Recommended Next Steps}

The highest-value next steps are:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Build a reproducible benchmark suite with explicit categories for
  mathematical, algorithmic, software, and semantically bridged claims.
  \item Report outcomes by evidence strength:
  machine-checked, SMT-proved, derived, formalized-unproved, and inconclusive.
  \item Expand the translation layer beyond narrow templates, especially for
  algorithmic correctness and program invariants.
  \item Compare necessity-first routing against pure hybrid proving as an
  ablation.
  \item Evaluate whether semantic bridging improves practical resolution quality
  without increasing false confidence.
  \item Continue repository hardening until the remaining style debt is gone
  and the benchmark path is fully automated.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}

Cognitive Dissonance DSPy is already more than a conceptual demo. It is a
nontrivial research prototype for proof-oriented conflict resolution in
multi-agent LLM systems, with a meaningful architecture, explicit proof-trust
semantics, real CI for Coq smoke coverage, and increasingly serious
reproducibility work.

Its strongest contribution is architectural and epistemic. The framework treats
formal verification as a first-class resolution policy and insists that proof
strength be represented honestly. Its main remaining work is empirical and
semantic: richer translation, stronger benchmarks, and better-justified
bridging from subjective language to objective verification targets.
