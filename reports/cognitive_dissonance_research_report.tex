\begin{abstract}
This report studies proof-first conflict resolution for formalizable agent
disagreements. The goal is not to argue that large language models broadly
solve disagreement through theorem proving. The goal is to evaluate where the
pipeline succeeds, where it fails, and which metrics are required to avoid
overstating capability.

The evaluation decomposes the task into four layers: deterministic
canonicalization, provider-assisted extraction, proof outcome, and evidence
strength. Three benchmark slices were run: a 45-case symbolic verification
benchmark, a 35-case easy natural-language extraction benchmark, and a 19-case
hard paraphrase benchmark designed to defeat deterministic normalization. With
\texttt{coqc} and \texttt{coqchk} available locally, the symbolic benchmark is
fully decisive, and the easy natural-language benchmark also saturates on its
formalizable slice. However, that easy extraction result is no longer a useful
test of provider capability because all 27 formalizable cases are solved by
deterministic canonicalization before any provider call.

The hard paraphrase benchmark carries the main extraction result. On 17
formalizable paraphrases, deterministic-only extraction reaches 0.0\% exact
canonical match, while provider-assisted extraction reaches 70.6\% exact
canonical match, 76.5\% decisive coverage, and 70.6\% decisive accuracy.
Provider lift is therefore real, but it is not safe to treat proof success as a
proxy for extraction fidelity. The same benchmark exposes false negatives,
instability, and silent correction of at least one false claim into a different
true canonical statement. The central conclusion is methodological: proof-first
systems should be audited with exact-match metrics and typed evidence states,
because extraction quality and proof quality are distinct capabilities.
\end{abstract}

\end{itemize}

\section{Problem Statement}

When multiple agents disagree, most multi-agent systems resolve the conflict by
debate, voting, ranking, or confidence arbitration. Those tools are reasonable
when the disputed content is subjective. They are weaker when the claim is
formalizable and could, in principle, be proved or refuted.

This work studies a narrower and more disciplined question: what does it take
to evaluate a proof-first disagreement resolver without conflating extraction
quality, proof quality, and confidence reporting? The key design decision in
this report is to separate the pipeline into stages and to evaluate those
stages explicitly.

\section{Contributions}

The work should be understood as a systems-and-evaluation contribution with
four concrete parts.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item A proof-first conflict-resolution pipeline for formalizable agent
  disagreements.
  \item An evaluation decomposition that separates deterministic
  canonicalization, provider-assisted extraction, proof outcome, and evidence
  strength.
  \item An exact-match audit layer that treats literal extraction fidelity as a
  first-class metric rather than inferring success from downstream proof.
  \item A typed evidence model that distinguishes \texttt{machine\_checked},
  solver-backed, refutational, and weaker derived outcomes.
\end{enumerate}

The report also contains a negative result that matters for paper framing:
necessity does not separate the symbolic conditions on the current benchmark
and should not be positioned as the paper's main novelty angle.

\section{Evaluation Framework}

\subsection{Pipeline Decomposition}

The evaluation isolates four layers:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Canonicalization:} can the system normalize the claim into a
  proof-ready form without calling a provider?
  \item \textbf{Provider-assisted extraction:} when deterministic rules fail,
  can a provider extract the correct canonical claim?
  \item \textbf{Proof outcome:} once the canonical claim is available, can the
  proof stack resolve it decisively?
  \item \textbf{Evidence strength:} if the claim resolves, how strong is the
  resulting evidence?
\end{enumerate}

This decomposition is the report's core methodological claim. Without it, a
system can appear successful because a proof succeeds on the wrong extracted
claim.

\subsection{Metrics}

The study reports:

\begin{itemize}
  \item direct translation success from raw natural language
  \item formalizability accuracy
  \item category accuracy
  \item exact canonical match
  \item translation success after extraction
  \item decisive coverage
  \item decisive accuracy
  \item machine-checked count
  \item semantic-drift count and semantic-drift decisive-error count
  \item repeated-trial stability on selected cases
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Evidence States}

The system distinguishes decisive and non-decisive outcomes instead of treating
all success labels as equivalent.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Decisive examples:} \texttt{machine\_checked},
  \texttt{smt\_proved}, \texttt{refuted}, \texttt{smt\_refuted}
  \item \textbf{Non-decisive examples:} \texttt{derived\_proved},
  \texttt{derived\_refuted}, \texttt{inconclusive}
\end{itemize}

This distinction is not cosmetic. It prevents the system from collapsing
``proved,'' ``refuted,'' and ``partially supported'' into the same headline
claim.

\subsection{Benchmarks}

\textbf{Symbolic verification benchmark.} 45 canonical claims across
arithmetic, multiplication, subtraction, factorial, Fibonacci, GCD,
inequalities, implication, universal quantification, and existential
quantification.

\textbf{Easy extraction benchmark.} 35 natural-language cases:

\begin{itemize}
  \item 27 formalizable claims
  \item 8 unformalizable controls
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Paraphrase stress benchmark.} 19 harder cases:

\begin{itemize}
  \item 17 formalizable paraphrases designed to evade deterministic rules
  \item 2 unformalizable controls
\end{itemize}

The stress set includes paraphrastic arithmetic, subtraction phrasing such as
``subtracting five from twelve,'' number words, and quantified or implication
forms such as ``there is an x greater than zero'' and ``any n is equal to
itself.''

\subsection{Repeated-Trial Probes}

Two repeated-trial probes were run:

\begin{itemize}
  \item a 6-case stability check over 2 trials per case on representative
  benchmark inputs
  \item a 5-case hard-failure probe over 10 trials per case on selected stress
  failures
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Interpretation.} This is the benchmark that carries the main extraction

\subsection{Stability and Silent Correction}

The representative 6-case stability check is fully stable over 2 trials per
case.

The selected hard-failure probe is not. Across 5 cases and 10 trials per case:

\begin{itemize}
  \item stable-case rate is 80.0\%
  \item one false arithmetic paraphrase flips between correct extraction and
  rejection
  \item one false subtraction paraphrase is stable but always wrong, rewritten
  into a different true fact
  \item quantified and implication paraphrases are stable false negatives
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Interpretation.} The central negative result is not just that provider
extraction misses some hard paraphrases. It is that extraction can be wrong in
a dangerous direction by silently converting a false claim into a different true
claim.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{What Is Actually Supported}

The current results support three claims.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Once a claim is correctly canonicalized, the proof stack is strong on
  the current internal suites.
  \item Provider-assisted extraction adds substantial lift on paraphrases that
  deterministic rules cannot handle.
  \item Exact-match auditing and evidence typing are necessary if the system is
  going to report results honestly.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{What Is Not Supported}

The current results do not support the following claims.

\begin{itemize}
  \item that multi-agent LLMs broadly resolve disagreement through proof
  \item that necessity-enhanced reasoning is the main novelty here
  \item that provider extraction is reliable on open-ended paraphrase variation
  \item that these benchmarks justify open-domain generalization claims
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Paper Framing}

The strongest paperable claim is methodological:

\begin{quote}
Proof-first conflict resolution should be evaluated with exact-match auditing
and typed evidence states, because extraction lift and proof strength are
distinct capabilities and they fail in different ways.
\end{quote}

That framing is stronger than a generic ``theorem-proving agents'' story
because it is directly supported by the hard paraphrase benchmark and the
silent-correction failure mode.

\section{Limitations}

\subsection{Internal Benchmarks}

The benchmarks are still repository-authored and relatively small. They are
useful for disciplined internal evaluation, not for strong open-domain claims.

\subsection{Benchmark-Aware Iteration}

The system was improved in response to observed benchmark failures. That is the
right engineering loop, but it also means the final results are partially tuned
against these benchmarks.

\subsection{Easy-Benchmark Saturation}

The easy extraction benchmark now functions as a calibration baseline rather
than as a discriminating test of provider extraction.

\subsection{Provider Drift}

The provider-backed path depends on one endpoint, one model, and one date.
Repeated trials already show instability on at least one hard paraphrase.

\subsection{Evidence Distribution}

Even where performance saturates, evidence strength varies. Only a subset of
resolved cases are machine-checked; many others are solver-backed or
refutational rather than proof-object verified.

\section{Conclusion}

The clean reading of the current work is not ``theorem-proving agents solve
disagreement.'' The clean reading is:

\begin{quote}
Canonical proof-first resolution is strong once the claim is in the right form,
but evaluation must separate extraction fidelity from proof success, and exact-
match auditing is necessary because provider lift on hard paraphrases comes
with silent-correction risk.
\end{quote}

That is a narrower claim than a broad agentic theorem-proving story, but it is
also a more defensible one. It gives the work a clear center: an evaluation
framework for proof-first conflict resolution that measures the right failure
modes instead of hiding them behind aggregate success rates.
