\begin{abstract}
This report evaluates the current state of Cognitive Dissonance DSPy as a
research artifact. The central question is whether a multi-agent LLM system can
resolve formalizable disagreements through proof instead of through debate or
confidence aggregation. To answer that question, I ran three experiments: an
expanded symbolic verification benchmark, a live natural-language extraction
benchmark against an OpenAI-compatible endpoint, and a harder paraphrase stress
benchmark designed to defeat the repository's deterministic normalization
rules.

The results separate into an easy story and a hard story. On the easy internal
benchmark, direct translation from raw natural language succeeds on only 22.2\%
of the formalizable cases, but deterministic canonicalization plus proof
resolve all 27 formalizable cases decisively. On that benchmark, the provider's
measured role is mostly triaging the 8 unformalizable controls. On the harder
paraphrase benchmark, deterministic-only extraction fails completely, while
provider-backed extraction lifts exact canonicalization to 70.6\%, decisive
coverage to 76.5\%, and decisive accuracy to 70.6\% on formalizable cases. The
same stress benchmark also exposes two important failure modes: stable false
negatives on quantified or implication paraphrases, and silent correction of at
least one false claim into a different true canonical fact. With
\texttt{coqc} and \texttt{coqchk} installed locally, the symbolic benchmark
saturates at 45/45 decisive outcomes. The strongest conclusion is therefore
methodological: proof-first routing is credible on canonical claims, but model-
driven extraction still requires exact-match auditing and harder benchmarks.
\end{abstract}

\section*{Executive Summary}

\begin{summarybox}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Symbolic verification baseline.} The symbolic benchmark now
  contains 45 claims across arithmetic, number theory, inequalities, and
  quantified logic, and both hybrid conditions reach 100.0\% decisive coverage
  and 100.0\% decisive accuracy with 13 machine-checked cases.
  \item \textbf{Easy extraction benchmark.} The main natural-language
  benchmark contains 35 cases, including 27 formalizable claims and 8
  unformalizable controls. Direct translation succeeds on only 6 of the 27
  formalizable cases, while extraction plus proof resolves all 27 decisively.
  \item \textbf{Provider provenance result.} All 27 formalizable cases in the
  easy benchmark are handled by deterministic canonicalization before any
  provider call. On that benchmark, the provider is mostly doing subjective
  triage.
  \item \textbf{Hard paraphrase result.} On a 19-case paraphrase stress slice,
  deterministic-only extraction reaches 0.0\% exact canonicalization on the 17
  formalizable cases, while provider-backed extraction reaches 70.6\% exact
  canonicalization and 70.6\% decisive accuracy.
  \item \textbf{Methodological result.} The harder benchmark shows why exact-
  match auditing matters: the provider can miss formalizable claims and can
  silently rewrite a false claim into a different true canonical statement.
\end{itemize}
\end{summarybox}

\section{Objective}

Most multi-agent systems handle disagreement with prompting, debate, voting, or
confidence aggregation. Those strategies are appropriate when the disagreement
is subjective. They are weaker when the disputed claim is formalizable.

This repository explores a different policy:

\begin{quote}
If a claim can be translated into a formal object, attempt proof before
arbitration.
\end{quote}

The goal of this study was not to restate the architecture, but to run a
larger benchmark, measure which subsystem is actually doing the work, and
rewrite the repository narrative around the observed results rather than around
older claims.

\section{Research Questions}

The study focused on five practical questions.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{RQ1:} Does necessity-enhanced verification materially improve
  symbolic claim handling on the expanded benchmark?
  \item \textbf{RQ2:} Does structured extraction and canonicalization improve
  translation success over direct pattern matching on natural-language claims?
  \item \textbf{RQ3:} After successful extraction, how much of the remaining
  problem is still in proof rather than extraction?
  \item \textbf{RQ4:} On the enlarged benchmark, how much of the extraction
  result truly depends on the provider rather than deterministic
  canonicalization?
  \item \textbf{RQ5:} On paraphrases that defeat deterministic rules, how much
  lift does provider-backed extraction add, and what failure modes remain?
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Evidence Policy}

The study intentionally separates \emph{decisive} and \emph{optimistic}
outcomes.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Decisive:} statuses strong enough to resolve truth or falsity
  directly, such as \texttt{machine\_checked}, \texttt{smt\_proved},
  \texttt{refuted}, and \texttt{smt\_refuted}.
  \item \textbf{Optimistic:} statuses that include derived support. These do
  not count as decisive ground truth.
\end{itemize}

This distinction is not bookkeeping. It is the core epistemic discipline of the
project.

\section{Benchmark Design}

\subsection{Formal Verification Benchmark}

The symbolic benchmark contains 45 claims across 10 families:

\begin{itemize}
  \item arithmetic
  \item multiplication
  \item subtraction
  \item factorial
  \item Fibonacci
  \item GCD
  \item inequalities
  \item implication
  \item universal quantification
  \item existential quantification
\end{itemize}

Each family includes true and false examples. The benchmark compares two
conditions:

\begin{itemize}
  \item hybrid verification with necessity enabled
  \item hybrid verification without necessity
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Extraction Benchmark}

The natural-language benchmark contains 35 cases:

\begin{itemize}
  \item 27 formalizable utterances
  \item 8 unformalizable subjective controls
\end{itemize}

The study measures:

\begin{itemize}
  \item direct translation from raw natural language
  \item structured extraction followed by translation and proof
  \item the provenance of extraction itself: deterministic fast-path versus
  provider-involved routing
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Paraphrase Stress Benchmark}

The harder paraphrase benchmark contains 19 cases:

\begin{itemize}
  \item 17 formalizable paraphrases written to evade deterministic fast-path
  normalization
  \item 2 unformalizable subjective controls
\end{itemize}

These cases intentionally include:

\begin{itemize}
  \item arithmetic and multiplication phrased as ``adding'' or ``the product
  of''
  \item subtraction phrased as ``subtracting five from twelve''
  \item number words such as ``forty-two'' and ``one hundred twenty''
  \item quantified and implication paraphrases such as ``there is an x greater
  than zero'' and ``any n is equal to itself''
\end{itemize}

The benchmark compares:

\begin{itemize}
  \item deterministic-only extraction with provider access disabled
  \item provider-enabled extraction at temperature 0.0
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Stability Checks}

A repeated-measures check runs extraction twice on 6 representative cases:

\begin{itemize}
  \item arithmetic true
  \item factorial base case
  \item inequality false
  \item implication false
  \item existential true
  \item subjective control
\end{itemize}

This is not a full calibration study. It is only a basic check for stable
temperature-0.0 behavior on representative inputs.

A separate failure probe runs 5 hard paraphrase cases over 10 repeated trials to
measure whether the observed failure modes are stable, unstable, or silently
wrong.

\section{Results}

\subsection{RQ1: Necessity and Symbolic Verification}

\begin{center}
\footnotesize
\begin{tabular}{lrrrrr}
\toprule
\textbf{Condition} & \textbf{Cases} & \textbf{Dec. cov.} & \textbf{Dec. acc.} & \textbf{M-check} & \textbf{Mean time} \\
\midrule
Hybrid + necessity & 45 & 100.0\% & 100.0\% & 13 & 133.4\,ms \\
Hybrid without necessity & 45 & 100.0\% & 100.0\% & 13 & 133.3\,ms \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\normalsize
\end{center}

The decisive status mix for the symbolic benchmark is the same in both
conditions:

\begin{itemize}
  \item 13 \texttt{machine\_checked}
  \item 10 \texttt{smt\_proved}
  \item 21 \texttt{refuted}
  \item 1 \texttt{smt\_refuted}
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Interpretation.} With Coq available locally, the expanded symbolic
benchmark is fully decisive in both conditions. Necessity therefore has no
measurable effect on headline accuracy or coverage on this suite. That does not
show necessity is unhelpful in general; it shows that the current benchmark no
longer separates the two conditions.

\subsection{RQ2: Extraction Versus Direct Translation}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lr}
\toprule
\textbf{Metric} & \textbf{Result} \\
\midrule
Formalizable natural-language cases & 27 \\
Direct translator success & 22.2\% \\
Extractor formalizability accuracy (all 35 cases) & 100.0\% \\
Extractor exact canonical match (formalizable cases) & 100.0\% \\
Translation success after extraction & 100.0\% \\
End-to-end decisive coverage after extraction & 100.0\% \\
End-to-end decisive accuracy after extraction & 100.0\% \\
Machine-checked formalizable cases after extraction & 7 \\
Mean extraction latency & 476.5\,ms/case \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\textbf{Interpretation.} Direct translation still fails on most raw natural
language inputs. Only 6 of the 27 formalizable cases translate directly, while
extraction plus proof resolves all 27 decisively. The extraction pipeline
therefore still matters on the expanded benchmark, but its useful work is now
mostly deterministic canonicalization rather than provider reasoning.

\subsection{RQ3: Proof After Successful Extraction}

After successful extraction, decisive proof coverage no longer remains limited:

\begin{itemize}
  \item end-to-end decisive coverage on formalizable extraction cases:
  100.0\%
  \item end-to-end decisive accuracy on formalizable extraction cases:
  100.0\%
\end{itemize}

The evidence mix on the 27 formalizable natural-language cases is:

\begin{itemize}
  \item 7 \texttt{machine\_checked}
  \item 7 \texttt{smt\_proved}
  \item 13 \texttt{refuted}
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Interpretation.} On the easy internal suite, proof is no longer the
measured bottleneck once Coq is installed locally. The remaining limitation is
not unresolved proof coverage on these canonical claims; it is whether harder
language preserves the same extraction behavior.

\subsection{RQ4: What Still Depends on the Provider on the Easy Suite?}

The extraction provenance instrumentation shows the following split across the
35-case natural-language benchmark:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lr}
\toprule
\textbf{Extraction mode} & \textbf{Count} \\
\midrule
Deterministic fast-path on formalizable cases & 27 \\
Provider extraction on formalizable cases & 0 \\
Provider triage on unformalizable controls & 8 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\textbf{Interpretation.} The benchmark no longer supports the strong claim that
provider extraction is the key enabler of formalizable claim normalization.
Instead, the measured result is that deterministic canonicalization handles all
formalizable cases in the current suite, while the provider is mainly used to
classify subjective controls as unformalizable.

\subsection{RQ5: Hard Paraphrases and Provider Failure Modes}

\begin{center}
\footnotesize
\begin{tabular}{lrr}
\toprule
\textbf{Metric} & \textbf{Det.-only} & \textbf{Provider} \\
\midrule
Direct translator success & 5.9\% & 5.9\% \\
Formalizability accuracy & 10.5\% & 78.9\% \\
Category accuracy & 0.0\% & 76.5\% \\
Exact canonical match & 0.0\% & 70.6\% \\
Translation success after extraction & 0.0\% & 76.5\% \\
Decisive coverage & 0.0\% & 76.5\% \\
Decisive accuracy & 0.0\% & 70.6\% \\
Machine-checked cases & 0 & 3 \\
Mean extraction latency & 0.03\,ms & 2998.2\,ms \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\normalsize
\end{center}

The provider-enabled stress run also records:

\begin{itemize}
  \item 4 formalizable false negatives
  \item 1 semantic-drift case
  \item 1 decisive error caused by semantic drift
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Interpretation.} This benchmark is the first one in the repository
where provider extraction adds essential lift rather than just triaging
subjective controls. That lift is real. However, the provider is still not
reliable enough to trust without literal-match auditing. The hard cases now
separate three distinct failure modes: rejection of formalizable logic
paraphrases, unstable behavior on some false arithmetic claims, and stable but
wrong silent correction on at least one subtraction paraphrase.

\subsection{Stability}

The repeated extraction check reached a 100.0\% stable-case rate over 6 cases
and 2 trials per case. This is a small result, but it suggests that the
temperature-0.0 extraction pipeline behaves deterministically on the selected
inputs.

The harder failure probe reached only an 80.0\% stable-case rate over 5 cases
and 10 trials per case. One false arithmetic paraphrase flipped between correct
formalization and outright rejection. Another false subtraction paraphrase was
stable but always wrong: it was rewritten into a different true subtraction
fact. The quantified and implication paraphrases were stable false negatives.

\section{Findings}

\subsection{Finding 1: The Easy Benchmarks Now Act As Calibration Baselines}

The enlarged symbolic benchmark reaches 45/45 decisive outcomes in both
conditions, and the easy natural-language benchmark reaches 27/27 decisive
outcomes on its formalizable slice. These suites are still useful, but they now
function more as calibration baselines than as discriminating research tests.

\subsection{Finding 2: Deterministic Canonicalization, Not the Provider, Drives the Easy Suite}

This is the most important change from earlier report drafts. The expanded
study now shows that all 27 formalizable natural-language cases are handled by
deterministic canonicalization. The provider remains useful, but its measured
role on this benchmark is primarily triaging the 8 unformalizable controls.

\subsection{Finding 3: Provider Extraction Adds Real Lift on Harder Paraphrases}

The paraphrase stress benchmark reverses the easy-suite conclusion. Once the
cases stop matching deterministic rules, provider-backed extraction becomes
necessary for any success at all. It lifts exact canonicalization from 0.0\% to
70.6\% and decisive coverage from 0.0\% to 76.5\% on formalizable cases.

\subsection{Finding 4: Provider Lift Is Real, But Silent Correction Is a Major Risk}

The stress benchmark shows that high-level correctness metrics are not enough.
At least one false claim is silently rewritten into a different true canonical
statement, which then proves successfully. That is worse than a clean false
negative because it creates a false appearance of precise extraction.

\subsection{Finding 5: Necessity Still Does Not Separate the Symbolic Conditions}

The expanded symbolic suite adds quantified logic and more number-theoretic
cases, but the headline result does not change: necessity and non-necessity
conditions still tie. The benchmark is therefore no longer useful for making a
novelty claim about necessity-based reasoning.

\subsection{Finding 6: Evidence Strength Still Varies Even When Accuracy Saturates}

The extraction benchmark resolves all 27 formalizable cases decisively, but
only 7 are machine-checked. The remainder are solver-backed proofs or decisive
refutations. The high headline number therefore still hides materially different
levels of evidential strength.

\subsection{Finding 7: The Honest Research Claim Is Methodological}

The strongest defensible contribution is not ``we built theorem-proving
agents.'' It is closer to this:

\begin{quote}
Explicit evidence typing and proof-first resolution can prevent a
multi-agent system from overstating certainty on formalizable disagreements,
even when the benchmark itself saturates.
\end{quote}

\section{Threats to Validity}

\subsection{Small Internal Benchmark}

The benchmark set is still small and repository-authored. It is useful for
engineering truth, but weak for strong claims about open-domain generalization.

\subsection{Benchmark-Aware Iteration}

This work used observed benchmark failures to improve the system and rerun the
study. That is the correct engineering loop, but it also means the final
results are partially the product of targeted iteration against the benchmark's
failure modes.

\subsection{Environment Sensitivity}

Installing Coq locally turned the earlier partial results into full decisive
coverage on this benchmark. Any report that ignores proof-toolchain
availability would therefore be misleading.

\subsection{Limited Language Breadth}

The formalizable natural-language suite is now dominated by claims that the
repository can canonicalize deterministically. That is good for reproducibility
and bad for making a broad model-centric extraction claim.

\subsection{Stress Benchmark Size}

The paraphrase stress benchmark exposes more realistic failure modes, but it is
still small. The provider-risk story is therefore credible as an internal
finding, not yet as a broad statistical claim.

\subsection{Provider Dependence Persists for Subjective Triage}

Although the provider no longer carries the formalizable slice on this suite,
the unformalizable control performance still depends on one provider
configuration on one date: OpenRouter-compatible routing with
\texttt{openai/gpt-4.1-mini} at temperature 0.0.

\subsection{Provider Behavior May Drift Over Time}

The same provider-backed extraction path showed instability on at least one hard
paraphrase over repeated temperature-0.0 trials. This means future runs may
shift even without code changes.

\section{Conclusion}

This repository now supports a narrower but more honest experimental claim:

\begin{quote}
On a small internal benchmark with Coq installed locally, Cognitive Dissonance
DSPy resolves canonical formalizable disagreements decisively end to end, and
provider-backed extraction adds real lift on harder paraphrases, but only under
exact-match auditing because silent correction remains a live failure mode.
\end{quote}

That is a credible systems result and a credible methodological result. It is
not yet a strong standalone novelty claim about open-ended theorem-proving
agents. The next research task is therefore no longer ``fix the current
benchmark.'' It is:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item expand the harder benchmark with broader paraphrase variation, more
  quantification, and less rule-aligned phrasing
  \item compare proof-first routing against debate, voting, and confidence
  arbitration baselines
  \item keep measuring evidence strength and literal extraction fidelity
  separately from headline accuracy
\end{enumerate}
