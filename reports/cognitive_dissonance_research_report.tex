\begin{abstract}
Proof-first conflict resolution is attractive for formalizable claim
disagreements, but its evaluation is easy to overstate. A system may appear
successful because its proof stack is strong, even when the extraction layer
silently changes the proposition under dispute. This paper studies that failure
mode directly. We evaluate Cognitive Dissonance DSPy by separating four layers:
deterministic canonicalization, provider-assisted extraction, proof outcome,
and evidence strength. We run three benchmark slices: a 45-case symbolic
verification benchmark, a 35-case easy natural-language extraction benchmark,
and a 19-case hard paraphrase benchmark designed to defeat deterministic
normalization.

Under the required \texttt{coqc}/\texttt{coqchk} toolchain, the symbolic
benchmark is fully decisive, and the easy extraction benchmark also saturates
on its formalizable slice. However, that easy benchmark no longer measures
provider extraction strength because all 27 formalizable cases are solved by
deterministic canonicalization before any provider call. The hard paraphrase
benchmark carries the main extraction result. On 17 formalizable paraphrases,
deterministic-only extraction reaches 0.0\% exact canonical match, while
provider-assisted extraction reaches 70.6\% exact canonical match, 76.5\%
decisive coverage, and 70.6\% decisive accuracy. The same benchmark also
produces false negatives, instability, and silent correction of at least one
false claim into a different true canonical statement.

The central conclusion is methodological: proof-first conflict resolution
should be evaluated with exact-match auditing and typed evidence states,
because extraction fidelity and proof success are distinct capabilities and
they fail in different ways.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

When a system must reconcile conflicting claims, common resolution strategies
include debate, voting, confidence arbitration, or judge-based aggregation.
These approaches are useful for subjective or underspecified disputes, but they
are weaker when the disputed content is formalizable and could in principle be
proved or refuted. A proof-first resolver offers a different contract: if a
claim can be reliably formalized, the system should prefer proof over
deliberation.

Recent progress in autoformalization and formal theorem proving makes that
contract plausible. Large language models have been used to translate natural
language mathematics into proof-assistant statements
\citep{wu2022autoformalization,azerbayev2023proofnet,zhang2025wild}, to drive
theorem proving environments such as LeanDojo \citep{yang2023leandojo}, and to
achieve strong performance on formal reasoning benchmarks such as FormalMATH
and MiniF2F with systems like DeepSeek-Prover-V2 and BFS-Prover
\citep{yu2025formalmath,ren2025deepseek,hsieh2025bfs}. At the same time,
recent evaluation work has highlighted that benchmark design and equivalence
metrics matter substantially for autoformalization quality
\citep{poiroux2025reliable,poiroux2025rlmeval}. This paper takes that concern
one step further in the context of disagreement resolution.

The key problem is that proof success alone is not enough. In a proof-first
pipeline, the system can fail before theorem proving begins: the extraction
layer may reject a formalizable claim, misclassify its category, or silently
replace it with a different canonical proposition. In the last case, a strong
proof backend can produce the wrong answer for the original dispute while still
appearing successful. For a disagreement resolver, that is a worse error than
a clean abstention.

This paper therefore centers evaluation methodology rather than raw proving
novelty. The main claim is not that large language models broadly resolve
disagreements through proof. The main claim is that proof-first conflict
resolution should be evaluated by separating deterministic canonicalization,
provider-assisted extraction, proof outcome, and evidence strength, and that
exact-match auditing is necessary because provider lift on hard paraphrases
comes with silent-correction risk.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
This paper makes four contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item It introduces an evaluation decomposition that separates
  canonicalization, provider extraction, proof outcome, and evidence strength.
  \item It defines an exact-match auditing protocol and shows that it is
  operationally necessary because proof success is not a safe proxy for
  extraction fidelity.
  \item It presents a hard paraphrase stress benchmark that exposes provider
  lift, false negatives, instability, and silent-correction errors in the same
  evaluation slice.
  \item It reports a typed evidence model that keeps machine-checked,
  solver-backed, refutational, and weaker derived outcomes distinct.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Autoformalization and Formal-Math Benchmarks}

Autoformalization with large language models has moved rapidly from proof of
concepts to benchmarked evaluation. \citet{wu2022autoformalization}
demonstrated that language models can translate natural-language mathematics
into Isabelle/HOL with enough fidelity to improve downstream theorem proving on
MiniF2F. ProofNet extended this line with a benchmark for undergraduate-level
autoformalization and formal proving \citep{azerbayev2023proofnet}. More
recent work has shown that curated benchmark performance can overstate
generalization: Autoformalization in the Wild evaluates real-world mathematical
definitions drawn from Wikipedia and arXiv and finds that structured grounding
and proof-assistant feedback matter materially \citep{zhang2025wild}.

Benchmark design itself has also become a research topic. ProofNetVerif
explicitly argues for more reliable evaluation of statement autoformalization
and introduces theorem-proving-based equivalence assessment rather than relying
only on coarse string comparison \citep{poiroux2025reliable}. RLMEval pushes in
the same direction for research-level neural theorem proving, arguing that
benchmark realism and evaluation methodology remain bottlenecks
\citep{poiroux2025rlmeval}. These works are directly relevant here, but our
focus differs: in conflict resolution, literal or canonical claim fidelity is
itself safety-critical because proposition substitution changes the object of
the dispute.

\subsection{Neural Theorem Proving}

On the proving side, systems and environments such as LeanDojo, BFS-Prover,
FormalMATH, and DeepSeek-Prover-V2 have shown substantial progress in formal
reasoning with Lean-based workflows \citep{yang2023leandojo,hsieh2025bfs,
yu2025formalmath,ren2025deepseek}. This literature establishes that strong
proof backends now exist. Our paper does not claim a new state of the art in
formal theorem proving. Instead, it studies how a proof-capable system should
be evaluated when natural-language extraction is part of the end-to-end path.

\subsection{Debate-Based Disagreement Resolution}

Multi-agent debate has emerged as an alternative way to improve reasoning by
encouraging divergent thought and adjudicating between agent arguments
\citep{liang2024mad}. Debate-style systems target a broader class of reasoning
problems than the formalizable slice we study here. Our contribution is not a
replacement for debate in general. It is a narrower claim that when the
disagreement is formalizable, proof-first routing should be evaluated as a
distinct resolution mode rather than collapsed into generic deliberation.

\subsection{Faithfulness in Hybrid LLM-Theorem-Proving Systems}

Recent work on faithful LLM-driven theorem proving for NLI explanations
emphasizes that hybrid symbolic systems must be evaluated not only by success
but by fidelity to the intended explanation or claim \citep{quan2025faithful}.
That perspective is closely aligned with our own. The difference is that our
setting is conflict resolution over formalizable claims, where exact-match
auditing becomes a hard requirement because the downstream verifier can validate
a shifted proposition.

\section{Method}

\subsection{Pipeline Decomposition}

We evaluate the system by separating four layers:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Deterministic canonicalization}: can the claim be normalized
  into proof-ready form without any provider call?
  \item \textbf{Provider-assisted extraction}: when deterministic rules fail,
  can the provider recover the correct canonical claim and category?
  \item \textbf{Proof outcome}: once the canonical claim is available, can the
  proof stack resolve it decisively?
  \item \textbf{Evidence strength}: if the claim resolves, what is the
  strength of the evidence used to resolve it?
\end{enumerate}

This decomposition is the central design choice of the paper. It prevents the
evaluation from rewarding a proof backend for succeeding on the wrong extracted
claim.

\subsection{Exact-Match Auditing}

The evaluation treats exact canonical match as a first-class metric. This is
deliberately strict. In some autoformalization settings, theorem-equivalence
metrics may be more permissive and more appropriate \citep{poiroux2025reliable}.
In our setting, however, the system is not simply trying to recover an
equivalent formal statement. It is attempting to resolve a specific disputed
proposition. If the extraction layer silently changes the proposition, the
result is operationally wrong even if the new proposition is true and provable.

\subsection{Evidence Typing}

The system distinguishes decisive from non-decisive outcomes rather than
flattening all successes into a generic ``proved'' label. Decisive labels
include \texttt{machine\_checked}, \texttt{smt\_proved}, \texttt{refuted}, and
\texttt{smt\_refuted}. Non-decisive labels include \texttt{derived\_proved},
\texttt{derived\_refuted}, and \texttt{inconclusive}. This typed evidence model
is part of the claimed contribution because it prevents the system from
overstating certainty.

\subsection{Benchmark Slices}

\textbf{Symbolic verification benchmark.} A 45-case benchmark of canonical
claims across arithmetic, multiplication, subtraction, factorial, Fibonacci,
GCD, inequalities, implication, universal quantification, and existential
quantification.

\textbf{Easy extraction benchmark.} A 35-case natural-language benchmark with
27 formalizable claims and 8 unformalizable controls. This slice retains
natural-language phrasing but remains close to the system's deterministic
comfort zone.

\textbf{Hard paraphrase benchmark.} A 19-case benchmark with 17 formalizable
paraphrases designed to evade deterministic rules and 2 unformalizable
controls. The formalizable cases include paraphrastic arithmetic, subtraction
phrases such as ``subtracting five from twelve,'' number words, and quantified
forms such as ``there is an x greater than zero'' and ``any n is equal to
itself.''

\subsection{Metrics and Repeated-Trial Probes}

We report:

\begin{itemize}
  \item direct translation success from raw natural language
  \item formalizability accuracy
  \item category accuracy
  \item exact canonical match
  \item translation success after extraction
  \item decisive coverage
  \item decisive accuracy
  \item machine-checked count
  \item semantic-drift count
  \item semantic-drift decisive-error count
\end{itemize}

Two repeated-trial probes complement the benchmark runs:

\begin{itemize}
  \item a 6-case stability check over 2 trials per case on representative
  inputs
  \item a 5-case hard-failure probe over 10 trials per case on selected stress
  failures
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Stability and Silent Correction}

The representative 6-case stability check is fully stable over 2 trials per
case.

The selected hard-failure probe is not. Across 5 cases and 10 trials per case:

\begin{itemize}
  \item stable-case rate is 80.0\%
  \item one false arithmetic paraphrase flips between correct extraction and
  rejection
  \item one false subtraction paraphrase is stable but always wrong, rewritten
  into a different true fact
  \item quantified and implication paraphrases are stable false negatives
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Interpretation.} The most important negative result is not merely that
the provider misses some hard paraphrases. It is that the extraction layer can
fail in a dangerous direction by silently converting a false disputed claim
into a different true canonical claim.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{What the Results Support}

The current experiments support three claims.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Once a claim is correctly canonicalized, the proof stack is strong on
  the current internal suites.
  \item Provider-assisted extraction adds real lift on paraphrases that defeat
  deterministic rules.
  \item Exact-match auditing and typed evidence states are necessary for honest
  evaluation of proof-first conflict resolution.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{What the Results Do Not Support}

The experiments do not support the following broader claims:

\begin{itemize}
  \item that LLM systems broadly resolve disagreement through proof
  \item that necessity-enhanced reasoning is the main novelty here
  \item that provider extraction is reliable on open-ended paraphrase variation
  \item that the current internal benchmarks justify open-domain performance
  claims
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Relationship to Prior Work}

The results align with recent autoformalization evaluation papers that warn
against over-interpreting curated benchmark success
\citep{poiroux2025reliable,poiroux2025rlmeval,zhang2025wild}. They also fit the
trajectory of strong formal theorem-proving systems: the back end is no longer
the only bottleneck \citep{yang2023leandojo,hsieh2025bfs,ren2025deepseek}. The
main open question in our setting is not whether proofs can be found once the
proposition is correct. It is whether the end-to-end system can preserve the
identity of the disputed proposition before handing it to the prover.

\subsection{Why This Is a Methodological Paper}

The strongest paperable claim is methodological, not grandiose:

\begin{quote}
Proof-first conflict resolution should be evaluated with exact-match auditing
and typed evidence states, because extraction lift and proof strength are
distinct capabilities and they fail in different ways.
\end{quote}

That framing is stronger than a generic ``theorem-proving agents'' story
because it is directly supported by the hard paraphrase benchmark and the
silent-correction failure mode.

\section{Limitations}

\subsection{Internal Benchmarks}

The benchmarks are still repository-authored and relatively small. They are
useful for disciplined internal evaluation, not for strong open-domain claims.

\subsection{Benchmark-Aware Iteration}

The system was improved in response to observed benchmark failures. That is the
right engineering loop, but it also means the final results are partially tuned
against these benchmarks.

\subsection{Easy-Benchmark Saturation}

The easy extraction benchmark now functions as a calibration baseline rather
than as a discriminating test of provider extraction.

\subsection{Provider Drift}

The provider-backed path depends on one endpoint, one model, and one date.
Repeated trials already show instability on at least one hard paraphrase.

\subsection{Evidence Distribution}

Even where headline accuracy saturates, evidence strength varies. Only a subset
of resolved cases are machine-checked; many others are solver-backed or
refutational rather than proof-object verified.

\section{Conclusion}

The clean reading of the current work is not ``theorem-proving agents solve
disagreement.'' The clean reading is:

\begin{quote}
Proof-first resolution is strong once the disputed claim is correctly
canonicalized, but evaluation must separate extraction fidelity from proof
success, and exact-match auditing is necessary because provider lift on hard
paraphrases comes with silent-correction risk.
\end{quote}

That is a narrower claim than a broad agentic theorem-proving story, but it is
also a more defensible one. It gives the paper a stable center: a methods
paper on proof-first claim resolution that measures the right failure modes
instead of hiding them behind aggregate success rates.
